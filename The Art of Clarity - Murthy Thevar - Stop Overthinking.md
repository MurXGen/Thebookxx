## Why we overthink (and why it feels productive)

Overthinking disguises itself as diligence. It feels like you're being careful. But most overthinking is just the same three thoughts on a loop, dressed up as analysis.

**Murthy Thevar** makes a sharp point in **The Art of Clarity**: confusion is rarely a lack of information. More often it's too much of it, with no structure. We keep adding — more research, more opinions, more scenarios — when the real move is to subtract.

## What The Art of Clarity does differently

Most books about focus tell you to try harder. **The Art of Clarity** by **Murthy Thevar** does the opposite — it tells you to carry less. The premise is that clarity is a skill of removal: strip away the noise, and the right decision is usually already visible underneath.

That reframing matters. It turns "I need to think about this more" into "what can I take off the table right now?" — which is a far more useful question.

## A simple 3-step clarity routine you can try today

Drawing on the ideas in **Murthy Thevar's** book, here's a routine I now use whenever I feel stuck:

**1. Name the actual decision.** Write it in one sentence. If you can't, you're overthinking a feeling, not a decision.

**2. Remove what doesn't matter.** List everything on your mind about it, then cross out anything that won't change the outcome. Usually most of it goes.

**3. Pause, then choose.** Give it one deliberate pause — a walk, a night's sleep — and then decide. Clarity tends to surface in the quiet, not the noise.

It's simple on purpose. That's the point **The Art of Clarity** keeps making: simple, repeatable, and done today beats perfect and endlessly delayed.

## How clearer thinking changes your work and your calm

When your thinking is clear, two things happen. Your decisions get faster and better, because you're choosing from what matters instead of everything. And your stress drops, because you're no longer carrying ten open loops at once.

That's the quiet promise behind **The Art of Clarity** by **Murthy Thevar** — better decisions and a calmer mind aren't separate goals. Clear thinking gives you both.
